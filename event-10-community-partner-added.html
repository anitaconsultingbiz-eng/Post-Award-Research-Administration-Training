Story Background

The project is growing beyond the university

The Community Access to Preventive Healthcare Initiative is entering a period of growth. Participant recruitment is increasing, and the project team wants to expand outreach efforts into additional communities.

Dr. Brown identifies Community Health Access Network, known as CHAN, as a potential partner. CHAN has deep community knowledge, trusted local relationships, and experience working with residents who may be difficult to reach through traditional university-led outreach.

Karen Mitchell, CHAN’s Executive Director, is interested in supporting the project. Her organization can help strengthen recruitment, coordinate community outreach, and connect the project with local leaders.

Before CHAN begins work, Marcus must answer an important administrative question: does CHAN’s proposed role look more like a contractor relationship or a subrecipient relationship?

Core Question

Not all external organizations are treated the same

When an outside organization receives project funds, Marcus cannot classify the relationship based on payment alone. He must review the nature of the work and follow the institution’s process for documenting the determination.

Contractor

A contractor provides routine goods or services in a commercial relationship.

  • Provides standard services
  • Operates in a commercial environment
  • Does not direct project activities
  • Does not make programmatic decisions

Subrecipient

A subrecipient performs a substantive portion of the sponsored project.

  • Contributes programmatic expertise
  • Participates in project implementation
  • Influences project outcomes
  • Assumes responsibility for part of the work

The classification affects the agreement structure, monitoring requirements, financial oversight, reporting expectations, and compliance responsibilities.

Decision Point

How should Marcus classify the relationship?

CHAN will support outreach strategy, participant recruitment, and community engagement. The organization brings expertise and will help carry out project objectives. What is the best classification?

Resolution

Marcus documents that CHAN appears to meet subrecipient characteristics

Marcus does not make this decision casually, and he does not treat the relationship as a subaward simply because CHAN will receive project funds. He reviews the proposed work and documents why CHAN appears to be carrying out part of the sponsored project rather than providing only routine services.

Next Step

Marcus coordinates the next steps for subaward routing and planning

Once CHAN appears to meet subrecipient characteristics, Marcus does not create or approve the subaward on his own. He coordinates with the appropriate institutional office, helps gather the required information, and makes sure the project team understands what must be in place before CHAN begins subrecipient work.

Role Boundary

Who does what may vary by institution

At some institutions, a departmental research administrator helps gather materials and track the process. A central sponsored programs, contracting, procurement, or subaward office may prepare, negotiate, issue, approve, or execute the agreement.

For this training, Marcus’s role is to recognize the issue, document the basis for the determination, coordinate with the correct office, and help ensure monitoring and reporting expectations are clear before work begins.

1. Document the determination basis

Marcus documents why CHAN appears to be a subrecipient instead of a contractor. The file should explain that CHAN will contribute programmatic expertise, support implementation, and help carry out part of the funded work.

2. Coordinate with the appropriate office

Marcus routes the matter through the institution’s process. Depending on local policy, this may involve sponsored programs, contracts, procurement, a subaward team, or another central office.

3. Gather the scope and budget

The project team must clearly describe CHAN’s scope of work, deliverables, timeline, budget, payment structure, and connection to the approved project goals.

4. Clarify reporting expectations

CHAN should know what reports are required, how often they are due, who receives them, and what information must be submitted before invoices can be reviewed and approved.

5. Plan for monitoring

Because the university remains accountable to the sponsor, Marcus helps identify how CHAN’s performance, spending, invoices, deliverables, and documentation will be monitored during the project.

6. Pause before work begins

CHAN should not begin subrecipient work until the appropriate agreement, approvals, reporting expectations, monitoring process, and documentation requirements are in place.

Why this matters

A subaward creates responsibilities for both the university and CHAN. The university remains accountable to the sponsor for the full award, even when another organization carries out part of the work.

Marcus’s work helps prevent the project from moving ahead informally before the relationship, scope, budget, reporting expectations, and monitoring responsibilities are documented through the proper institutional process.

Common Misconceptions

What learners should not assume

“Any organization receiving money is a subrecipient.”

Payment alone does not determine classification. The nature of the work is the determining factor.

“Community organizations are always contractors.”

Community organizations can function as contractors or subrecipients depending on their project role.

“This is mainly a financial decision.”

Subrecipient determination is based on project responsibilities, participation, programmatic expertise, and relationship to project outcomes.

2 CFR 200.331 — Subrecipient and Contractor Determinations

This federal requirement explains that pass-through entities must make case-by-case determinations about whether another organization is acting as a subrecipient or a contractor.

Connection to Event 10: CHAN’s classification depends on the nature of its proposed work, not simply on the fact that it will be paid.

2 CFR 200.332 — Requirements for Pass-Through Entities

This federal requirement explains responsibilities that apply when a pass-through entity provides federal funds to a subrecipient, including award information, risk review, monitoring, and follow-up expectations.

Connection to Event 10: if CHAN is treated as a subrecipient, Crescent State University has monitoring responsibilities.
