Event 12

Late Cost Transfer

As Year 2 comes to a close, Marcus reviews a request to move older outreach expenses onto the grant. The costs may be project-related, but the timing creates audit risk.

Year 2 Event 12 Cost Transfer Review

Story Background

A correction request arrives four months later

The monitoring issue with CHAN has been resolved, and the project continues to make progress. Outreach activity has expanded, participant recruitment is active, and the project is approaching the end of Year 2.

Marcus receives a cost transfer request from a departmental administrator. Several expenses related to community outreach activities were originally charged to a departmental operating account instead of the Community Access grant.

The expenses occurred four months earlier. The administrator now asks whether the charges can be moved to the grant.

Marcus does not simply approve or deny the request on the spot. His role is to gather the facts, review the support, confirm the reason for the delayed correction, and route the request through the appropriate institutional process.

Trigger Issue

The department requests a late transfer

From: Department Administrator

To: Marcus Rivera

Subject: Cost Transfer Request – Outreach Expenses

Hi Marcus,

We identified several outreach-related expenses that were charged to the department operating account earlier this year. These expenses supported the Community Access to Preventive Healthcare Initiative.

The expenses include outreach materials, community event supplies, and printing costs used for participant recruitment. The charges occurred approximately four months ago.

Could these expenses be transferred to the Community Access grant? I have attached the original expense detail and a draft transfer request form.

Thank you,
Department Administrator

Training Concept

Cost transfers receive close review

A cost transfer is a reassignment of an expense from one account to another after the original transaction has already posted. Cost transfers can be legitimate corrections, but late or poorly explained transfers can create audit concerns.

Allowability

Is the cost permitted under sponsor, institutional, and regulatory requirements?

Allocability

Does the cost directly benefit the project being charged?

Documentation

Do records explain the reason for the transfer and show why the expense belongs on the receiving account?

Timing

The longer the delay, the more important the explanation and supporting documentation become.

Audit Risk

Late transfers may receive heightened scrutiny from auditors and sponsors.

Administrative judgment

Marcus reviews whether the request appears to correct a legitimate error and whether the documentation is strong enough to support routing the correction forward.

What Marcus Reviews

The transfer request needs more than “the grant has money”

Marcus’s review is a fact-gathering and documentation step. He checks whether the request has enough support to proceed through the institutional cost transfer process.

Original Expense Detail Report

Shows what was purchased, when the expense occurred, and where the cost originally posted.

Cost Transfer Request Form

Identifies the expense being moved, the original account, the receiving grant account, and the requested correction.

Justification Statement

Explains why the original charge was incorrect, why the expense belongs on the grant, and why the correction was not made earlier.

Project Activity Support

Connects the outreach supplies, printing, or materials to actual project activities.

Timing Explanation

Explains why the error was discovered four months after the original transaction.

Approval Routing

Confirms who must review and approve the correction under institutional cost transfer procedures.

Marcus’s Analysis

What would strengthen or weaken the request?

Stronger justification

  • Administrative coding error
  • Documentation showing project benefit
  • Outreach event records
  • Receipts or expense details
  • Explanation for why the error was discovered late
  • Evidence the transfer is not being made because funds are available

Weak or risky justification

  • “We forgot to charge the grant.”
  • “The grant still has money available.”
  • “The project is ending soon.”
  • No explanation for the delay
  • No documentation tying the expenses to project activity
  • Pattern of repeated late transfers

Available funding is not a valid reason for a transfer. The transfer must correct a legitimate error and be supported by documentation.

Decision Point

What should Marcus recommend?

The expenses appear related to community outreach, but the request was submitted four months after the original charges occurred. What should Marcus recommend before the transfer moves forward?

Resolution

Marcus documents the review and routes the correction

Marcus determines that the request has enough support to proceed through institutional review because the documentation connects the expenses to the project, explains the original coding error, and addresses the delay. The correction still follows the institution’s cost transfer approval process.

Cost Transfer Review Notes

Without supporting documentation, the same request might not move forward. Documentation is what turns a questionable late transfer into a defensible correction request.

Common Misconceptions

What learners should not assume

“If the project benefited, the charge can always be moved.”

Benefit alone is not enough. The transfer also needs a clear explanation, proper timing support, and documentation.

“Cost transfers are routine accounting actions.”

Transfers often receive heightened review because they can create audit concerns, especially when they are late.

“Available funding is a valid reason for a transfer.”

Transfers must correct legitimate errors. They should not be used to move costs because a grant has money available.

Resources

References behind this scenario

These references show why cost transfers require careful review and documentation. A post-award research administrator should evaluate whether the cost is allowable, allocable, supported, timely explained, and routed through the institution’s required correction process.

2 CFR 200.403 — Factors Affecting Allowability

This federal cost principle explains that costs charged to a federal award must meet allowability standards, including being necessary, reasonable, allocable, consistently treated, documented, and compliant with applicable requirements.

Connection to Event 12: the transferred expenses must still meet allowability requirements before they can be charged to the grant.

2 CFR 200.405 — Allocable Costs

This federal cost principle explains that a cost must benefit the specific award being charged and be assigned in proportion to the benefit received.

Connection to Event 12: the costs must belong on the Community Access grant, not just be convenient to move.

2 CFR 200.303 — Internal Controls

This federal requirement frames the institution’s responsibility to maintain controls that help prevent, detect, and correct errors in federal award activity.

Connection to Event 12: strong controls reduce the need for late corrections and help explain how errors are identified.

Institutional Cost Transfer Policy

Institutional cost transfer policy identifies timing rules, justification requirements, documentation standards, required approvals, and who has authority to approve or post the correction.

Connection to Event 12: Marcus must follow institutional policy before the transfer can move forward.
