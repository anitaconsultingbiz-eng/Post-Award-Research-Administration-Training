Event 05

Questionable Expense

Marcus finds a restaurant charge on the award. Before deciding whether it can remain on the grant, he needs to understand the purpose, documentation, and cost principles.

Year 1 Event 05 Documentation Review

Story Scene

A small charge raises a bigger question

During a routine financial review, Marcus identifies a $325 restaurant charge posted to the Community Access to Preventive Healthcare Initiative.

The transaction description reads: “Community Outreach Working Lunch.” At first, the amount does not seem large compared with the full award budget.

But Marcus knows that the size of the charge is not the only issue. Food expenses often require additional scrutiny because they can be difficult to connect clearly to the approved project purpose.

Marcus’s question is not, “Did money get spent?” His question is: “Should the project pay for it?”

Trigger Issue

A restaurant charge appears on the project account

Questionable does not automatically mean unallowable. It means Marcus needs to pause, review the documentation, and determine whether the expense meets the rules for the award.

This is how post-award problem-solving often works: Finance identifies a concern, the administrator gathers documentation, and the PI provides project context.

Training Concept

What makes an expense questionable?

A questionable expense is not automatically wrong. It is an expense that needs closer review because the purpose, benefit, documentation, or policy basis is not immediately clear.

Allowability

Is the cost permitted under the award terms, federal cost principles, and institutional policy?

Allocability

Does the cost benefit the project being charged?

Reasonableness

Would a prudent person consider the cost necessary and appropriate under the circumstances?

Documentation

Is there enough support to explain who attended, why the meeting occurred, and how it benefited the project?

Extra scrutiny

Meals, entertainment, alcohol, and hospitality-type costs often require careful review before remaining on a sponsored award.

Administrative goal

Marcus is not looking for a reason to reject the charge. He is looking for enough evidence to make and document the correct decision.

Marcus’s Expense Review

Marcus uses a documentation-first approach

Expense Review Framework

Can the restaurant charge remain on the grant?

Review required

Marcus does not approve or remove the charge based on the transaction title alone. He reviews whether the expense is tied to the approved project and supported by documentation.

1. What was purchased?

Marcus reviews the receipt to confirm the charge details and identify any items that may require special review.

2. Who attended?

Marcus checks whether the attendees were connected to project outreach or other approved project activities.

3. Why did the meeting occur?

Marcus reviews the justification memo to understand the business purpose of the working lunch.

4. How did it benefit the project?

Marcus determines whether the lunch directly supported community outreach, recruitment planning, or another project objective.

5. Is the cost reasonable?

Marcus considers whether the amount appears appropriate for the meeting purpose and number of attendees.

6. Is policy support clear?

Marcus checks institutional policy, award terms, and federal cost principles before making a decision.

If the documentation does not clearly support the charge, Marcus should not leave the expense on the grant without follow-up.

Administrator Thinking

What Marcus notices

The restaurant charge may have a legitimate project purpose. A working lunch with community partners could support outreach planning if it is properly documented and allowed by policy.

But the description alone is not enough. Marcus needs to understand the purpose, attendees, project benefit, and policy basis.

What supports the charge?

  • The description references community outreach
  • Community outreach is connected to the project
  • The cost may support planning or partner engagement

What creates concern?

  • The charge is for a restaurant
  • Food costs often require additional scrutiny
  • The project benefit is not clear from the transaction alone
  • Documentation must support allowability

Marcus should not assume the expense is unallowable, but he also should not assume it is allowable without evidence.

Key Takeaway

The question is whether the project should pay

Expense review is not just about confirming that money was spent. Marcus must determine whether the cost is allowable, allocable, reasonable, and properly documented before it remains on the grant.

Resources

References behind this scenario

These references show why Marcus cannot decide the restaurant charge based only on the dollar amount or the fact that a meeting occurred. A post-award research administrator must connect the expense to the project purpose, federal cost principles, institutional expense rules, sponsor terms, and supporting documentation.

2 CFR 200.403 — Factors Affecting Allowability

This federal cost principle explains the basic requirements a cost must meet before it can be charged to a federal award, including being necessary, reasonable, allocable, documented, consistently treated, and compliant with applicable requirements.

Connection to Event 05: Marcus must determine whether the restaurant charge can remain on the grant.

2 CFR 200.404 — Reasonable Costs

This section helps frame whether the amount and nature of the expense make sense under the circumstances. Marcus must consider whether the $325 charge is appropriate for the meeting purpose, number of attendees, and project benefit.

Connection to Event 05: Marcus must consider whether the restaurant charge is reasonable for the purpose and attendees.

2 CFR 200.405 — Allocable Costs

This federal cost principle explains that a cost must benefit the specific award being charged. Marcus needs to determine whether the working lunch directly supported project outreach or another approved project activity.

Connection to Event 05: Marcus must connect the working lunch to project outreach or another approved project activity.

Institutional Expense Policy

The institution’s expense policy sets local requirements for meals, business meetings, receipts, attendee lists, approvals, alcohol restrictions, and documentation. Even a project-related expense must follow institutional rules.

Connection to Event 05: food expenses often require specific documentation under institutional policy.

Award Terms and Conditions

Award terms may restrict certain categories of costs or require sponsor approval before they can be charged. Marcus needs to check whether the sponsor has placed limits on meals, meetings, or participant-related expenses.

Connection to Event 05: sponsor terms may limit what types of costs can be charged to the award.

Project Documentation

The project file should explain the business purpose of the expense, who attended, what was discussed, how the meeting supported the award, and whether required approvals and receipts are present.

Connection to Event 05: the decision depends on whether documentation supports the charge.
