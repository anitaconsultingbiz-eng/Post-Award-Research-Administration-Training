Year 1 Overview

Award Startup and Foundational Controls

Year 1 follows the first stage of Dr. Aneka Brown’s funded public health project. The award has been issued, the team is ready to begin, and Marcus Rivera must help determine what can move forward, what must pause, and what needs to be documented.

Events 01–07 Startup Year Post-Award Foundations

The Story Begins

A funded project is not the same as a ready project

Dr. Brown began with a public health problem: residents in underserved communities were often seeking medical care only after their health conditions had become serious.

She developed a research proposal to study barriers to preventive healthcare access and received a three-year, $350,000 NIH award. For the project team, the award feels like the green light they have been waiting for.

But for Marcus Rivera, the post-award research administrator, the award is only the beginning. Before the project can move at full speed, Marcus must review approvals, award terms, budget activity, staffing plans, documentation, and internal controls.

Year 1 asks one main question: Can the project begin responsibly?

People in the Startup Year

The first year centers on Dr. Brown and Marcus

Principal Investigator

Dr. Aneka Brown

Dr. Brown leads the scientific direction of the project. She is ready to begin recruitment and community-facing work, but project activity must still follow sponsor, compliance, and institutional requirements.

Post-Award Research Administrator

Marcus Rivera

Marcus helps the project move forward responsibly by reviewing award documents, monitoring financial activity, identifying risks, gathering information, and documenting next steps.

Year 1 Story Sequence

How the startup year unfolds

The Year 1 events are connected. Each event adds another layer to the startup record and shows how post-award administration protects the project before problems become larger.

01

Human Subjects Compliance Delay

Dr. Brown is ready to begin recruitment, but Marcus discovers that final IRB approval is still pending. The project is funded, but participant-facing work cannot begin yet.

02

Hiring Delay

A key project position remains vacant. At first, the unspent salary looks like savings, but Marcus recognizes that delayed hiring may signal delayed project activity.

03

Burn Rate Problem

Spending is lower than expected. Marcus reviews the budget activity with the project timeline to understand whether the award is progressing as planned.

04

Conference Opportunity

Dr. Brown wants to attend a conference using award funds. Marcus reviews whether the travel supports the approved project purpose and whether the documentation is strong enough.

05

Questionable Expenses

A restaurant charge appears on the project. Marcus does not assume it is wrong, but he checks whether the cost is reasonable, allocable, documented, and tied to project work.

06

Unexpected Equipment Purchase

The team needs tablets for data collection, but the purchase was not in the original budget. Marcus reviews whether the need is allowable, properly categorized, and requires additional approval.

07

Participant Incentive Question

Participant activity increases, and incentive records must be reconciled. Marcus helps identify whether the issue is a control problem, a documentation gap, or a correctable filing error.

★

Year 1 Capstone

The learner looks across the first year and connects compliance approvals, budget review, spending decisions, documentation, and internal controls into one startup narrative.

Post-Award Lens

What learners should notice in Year 1

The startup year teaches that research administration is not just reacting to problems. It is watching for early signals, asking the right questions, gathering the right records, and helping the team move forward without creating avoidable risk.

Marcus is not the scientific lead, the finance office, the IRB, or the sponsor. His role is to connect the administrative pieces: what the award allows, what the project is doing, what the records show, and what needs to happen next.

By the end of Year 1, learners should understand that a funded project needs controls before it needs speed.
