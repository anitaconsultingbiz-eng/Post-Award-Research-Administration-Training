Year 2 Review

The project is active, but active does not mean automatic

By the end of Year 2, the Community Access to Preventive Healthcare Initiative is no longer a new project. Recruitment is active, community outreach has expanded, CHAN has joined the work through a subaward, and project spending is more complex.

Dr. Brown continues to lead the scientific work. Jasmine supports day-to-day operations. David reviews financial activity and payment-related issues. Karen Mitchell and CHAN bring community expertise. Marcus helps connect the administrative pieces by gathering information, reviewing documentation, coordinating with the appropriate offices, and watching for risks.

The Year 2 events were not separate problems. Together, they show that active award management requires continuous monitoring, documentation, communication, coordination, and judgment.

Year 1 asked whether the project could start. Year 2 asks whether the active project is being managed responsibly.

Year 2 Story Arc

What happened across the second year

Year 2 showed what happens after a project becomes active. The issues were no longer only about whether work could begin. They were about whether the people, partners, charges, documentation, and corrections still matched the approved award.

08

Payroll Distribution Error

David identified a salary charge that did not appear to match Jasmine’s approved salary allocation. Marcus reviewed the award documentation and helped gather the support needed for correction review.

Lesson: Payroll monitoring checks whether salary charges are posted correctly and supported by records.
09

Effort Change

Dr. Brown took on new university-wide responsibilities, raising questions about whether her project effort still matched sponsor commitments.

Lesson: Salary and effort are related, but they are not the same thing.
10

Community Partner Added

CHAN entered the project, and Marcus reviewed whether the organization’s proposed role appeared to meet subrecipient characteristics rather than routine contractor services.

Lesson: The nature of the work determines the relationship, and institutional routing may be required before work begins.
11

Subrecipient Monitoring Problem

David flagged CHAN’s invoice package because the required quarterly progress report was missing. Marcus followed up with Karen, documented the monitoring action, and updated Research Finance once the package was complete from the monitoring side.

Lesson: Monitoring includes financial review and performance review.
12

Late Cost Transfer

A department requested that older outreach expenses be moved to the grant four months after the original charges occurred. Marcus reviewed the support and routed the request through the institutional cost transfer process.

Lesson: Late corrections require strong documentation, justification, and proper approval routing.

Capstone Activity

Prepare a Year 2 administrative assessment

In this capstone, you step into Marcus’s role and review the overall status of the award at the end of Year 2. The goal is not to memorize the events. The goal is to explain what the events reveal about the project’s financial health, personnel management, subrecipient oversight, compliance activity, documentation, and administrative risk.

1. Review payroll accuracy

Did salary charges match approved allocations, project work, and supporting records?

2. Review effort commitments

Did key personnel involvement still align with sponsor commitments and project needs?

3. Review external partnerships

Was CHAN’s role reviewed correctly, and were the appropriate agreement, routing, and oversight expectations identified?

4. Review subrecipient monitoring

Were invoices, reports, deliverables, and performance records reviewed before payment moved forward?

5. Review financial corrections

Were cost transfers supported, timely explained, and routed through the appropriate process?

6. Think forward

Which Year 2 issues could become larger Year 3 risks if not monitored carefully?

Administrator Toolkit

The Year 2 review frameworks

Marcus used several repeatable review frameworks throughout Year 2. These frameworks helped him move beyond isolated transactions and evaluate the health of the active award.

Payroll Review

  • Where was salary charged?
  • Does the charge match the approved allocation?
  • Does the work directly benefit the project?
  • Is a correction review required?

Effort Review

  • What effort was promised?
  • What work is currently being performed?
  • Did responsibilities change?
  • Is sponsor notification or approval required?

Subrecipient Determination

  • Is the organization performing project work?
  • Is it contributing programmatic expertise?
  • Does it influence project outcomes?
  • Does the role appear to meet subrecipient characteristics?

Subrecipient Monitoring

  • Does the invoice match the subaward?
  • Are required reports included?
  • Is performance progress documented?
  • Are risk indicators present?

Cost Transfer Review

  • What error is being corrected?
  • Why was the original charge wrong?
  • Why is the correction late?
  • Does documentation support routing the transfer?

Risk Review

  • Is this a one-time issue or a pattern?
  • Could this affect reporting?
  • Could this create audit findings?
  • What should be monitored next?

Marcus’s Year 2 pattern: compare records, confirm commitments, gather missing information, coordinate with the right offices, document the basis for action, and monitor what could become a future risk.

Year 2 Mindset

The pattern Marcus used throughout the active award year

Compare records

Marcus compares payroll reports, effort records, subaward documents, invoices, progress reports, and transfer requests.

Confirm commitments

Marcus checks whether project work, personnel effort, and partner responsibilities still match what was approved.

Ask before assuming

Marcus requests missing information before recommending, correcting, rejecting, routing, or escalating an issue.

Document the basis

Marcus documents why a correction, classification review, routing decision, or follow-up action is appropriate.

Monitor patterns

Marcus looks for recurring issues that may signal a control, communication, or training problem.

Protect relationships and compliance

Marcus supports the project team and partners while protecting the award, the institution, and the sponsor’s expectations.

Year 2 shows that post-award administration is not just transaction review. It is active stewardship of an award that is changing over time.

Transition to Year 3

From active management to closeout readiness

By the end of Year 2, the project is active, complex, and connected to external partners. Year 3 will require Marcus to think about final spending, reporting, deliverables, unresolved issues, and closeout readiness.

Final spending

Year 3 will require closer review of whether remaining funds are spent appropriately and on time.

Final reports

Sponsors will expect accurate financial, performance, and administrative reporting.

Subrecipient closeout

CHAN’s final invoices, reports, and deliverables will need to be reviewed before closeout.

Unresolved corrections

Any late transfers, payroll adjustments, or documentation gaps must be resolved before the award closes.

Audit readiness

The project file should explain what happened, what was reviewed, and why decisions were made.

More pressure near the end

As the end date approaches, rushed decisions and incomplete documentation can create risk.

Year 2 asked: “Are we managing the active project appropriately?” Year 3 will increasingly ask: “Are we ready to close this award cleanly?”
